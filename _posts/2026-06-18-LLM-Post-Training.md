# 1. Introduction

The remarkable capabilities of modern large language models (LLMs) are the result of a multi-stage training pipeline. After large-scale pretraining on internet-scale text corpora, models are typically refined through a post-training stage designed to align their behavior with human expectations and task-specific objectives. Early post-training approaches relied primarily on supervised fine-tuning (SFT), where models were trained to imitate high-quality demonstrations produced by human annotators. While SFT significantly improves instruction following and conversational abilities, recent advances in reasoning models have highlighted its limitations. These developments suggest that the most substantial improvements in complex reasoning may arise not merely from better imitation, but from more sophisticated post-training strategies that leverage feedback and preference information.

The fundamental limitation of supervised fine-tuning is that it optimizes the probability of reproducing demonstration trajectories rather than the quality of the final outcomes. In many reasoning tasks, there may exist multiple valid chains of thought leading to the same correct answer, yet SFT only reinforces the specific trajectories present in the training data. Furthermore, during inference, language models generate responses autoregressively by conditioning on their own previously generated tokens, creating a mismatch between training and deployment known as exposure bias. As errors accumulate, the model is forced into regions of the state space that were rarely encountered during supervised training. Consequently, imitation alone may be insufficient for developing robust reasoning capabilities.

To overcome these limitations, modern LLM post-training has evolved beyond supervised fine-tuning into methods that optimize models using richer forms of feedback. Broadly speaking, these methods can be divided into two categories. The first formulates language generation as a reinforcement learning problem, allowing models to generate candidate responses, evaluate them using reward signals, and improve their behavior through policy optimization. The second relies on preference optimization, where models are trained directly from pairwise preference data without explicitly learning a reward function or interacting with an environment. Although these paradigms differ substantially in their optimization procedures, they share a common objective: moving beyond imitation to optimize model behavior according to human preferences and desired outcomes.

This article presents a mathematical overview of modern LLM post-training. We begin with supervised fine-tuning (SFT), which serves as the foundation of nearly all contemporary post-training pipelines, and examine both its strengths and limitations. We then introduce reinforcement learning as a principled framework for optimizing language models beyond imitation, beginning with the policy gradient theorem and progressing through Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), and On-Policy Distillation (OPD). Finally, we explore Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), which approaches the same post-training problem from a different perspective by optimizing language models directly from preference data without explicit reward modeling. Together, these methods illustrate the evolution of LLM post-training from supervised imitation toward increasingly effective forms of feedback-driven optimization, providing the mathematical foundations underlying many of today's most capable reasoning models.
