# 1. Introduction

The remarkable capabilities of modern large language models (LLMs) are the result of a multi-stage training pipeline. After large-scale pretraining on internet-scale text corpora, models are typically refined through a post-training stage designed to align their behavior with human expectations and task-specific objectives. Early post-training approaches relied primarily on supervised fine-tuning (SFT), where models were trained to imitate high-quality demonstrations produced by human annotators. While SFT significantly improves instruction following and conversational abilities, much of the recent progress in reasoning has been driven by reinforcement learning (RL) based post-training methods. These methods leverage feedback or reward signals to optimize the model's policy based on the quality of its generated responses. Alongside reinforcement learning, preference optimization has emerged as another important paradigm, where models are trained directly from human preference data to better align their outputs with human choices, without explicitly relying on reinforcement learning.

Although supervised fine-tuning provides a strong initialization for language models, it fundamentally optimizes the probability of reproducing demonstration trajectories rather than the quality of the final outcomes. In many reasoning tasks, there may exist multiple valid chains of thought leading to the same correct answer, yet SFT only reinforces the specific trajectories present in the training data. Furthermore, during inference, language models generate responses autoregressively by conditioning on their own previously generated tokens, creating a mismatch between training and deployment known as exposure bias. As errors accumulate, the model is forced into regions of the state space that were rarely encountered during supervised training. Consequently, imitation alone may be insufficient for developing robust reasoning capabilities, motivating the development of more advanced post-training algorithms.

Reinforcement learning addresses these limitations by optimizing models directly with respect to the quality of their generated outputs rather than their ability to reproduce demonstrations. Instead of asking, "Can the model reproduce this demonstration?", reinforcement learning asks, "Did the model produce a desirable outcome?" This distinction is particularly important for reasoning tasks, where multiple reasoning trajectories may lead to the same correct answer. By allowing models to generate candidate responses, evaluate them using reward signals, and update their policy based on the observed outcomes, reinforcement learning enables models to discover behaviors that extend beyond those explicitly represented in the training data. Preference optimization, on the other hand, approaches the same problem from a different perspective by learning directly from pairwise human preferences, avoiding the need for explicit reward modeling or policy optimization while still aligning model behavior with human judgments.

This article presents a mathematical overview of the major post-training methods used in modern large language models. We begin with supervised fine-tuning (SFT), the foundation upon which nearly all contemporary post-training pipelines are built, and examine both its strengths and limitations. We then introduce the mathematical foundations of reinforcement learning through policy gradient methods before progressively developing Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), and On-Policy Distillation (OPD), highlighting the key ideas that motivated each successive algorithm. Finally, we examine Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), one of the most influential preference optimization methods, and derive its objective from first principles. Together, these methods illustrate the evolution of LLM post-training from supervised imitation to optimization driven by rewards and human preferences, providing the mathematical foundations behind many of today's most capable language models.
