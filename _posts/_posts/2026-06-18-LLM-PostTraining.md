# 1. Introduction

The remarkable capabilities of modern large language models (LLMs) are the result of a multi-stage training pipeline. After large-scale pretraining on internet-scale text corpora, models are typically refined through a post-training stage designed to align their behavior with human expectations and task-specific objectives. Early post-training approaches relied primarily on supervised fine-tuning (SFT), where models were trained to imitate high-quality demonstrations produced by human annotators. While SFT significantly improves instruction following and conversational abilities, recent advances in reasoning models have highlighted its limitations. Models such as DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI's reasoning systems suggest that the most substantial improvements in complex reasoning may arise not from better imitation, but from learning through interaction and feedback.

The fundamental limitation of supervised fine-tuning is that it optimizes the probability of reproducing demonstration trajectories rather than the quality of the final outcomes. In many reasoning tasks, there may exist multiple valid chains of thought leading to the same correct answer, yet SFT only reinforces the specific trajectories present in the training data. Furthermore, during inference, language models generate responses autoregressively by conditioning on their own previously generated tokens, creating a mismatch between training and deployment known as exposure bias. As errors accumulate, the model is forced into regions of the state space that were rarely encountered during supervised training. Consequently, imitation alone may be insufficient for developing robust reasoning capabilities.

Reinforcement learning offers an alternative paradigm in which models are optimized directly with respect to the quality of their generated outputs. Instead of asking, "Can the model reproduce this demonstration?", reinforcement learning asks, "Did the model achieve a desirable outcome?" This distinction is particularly important for reasoning tasks, where intermediate steps may vary substantially even when the final answer is correct. By allowing models to generate candidate solutions, evaluate their consequences through reward signals, and update their behavior based on these outcomes, reinforcement learning provides a mechanism for discovering strategies that extend beyond those explicitly represented in the training data. Moreover, because policy optimization is performed using samples generated by the current model, reinforcement learning naturally adapts to the distribution induced by the model itself.

This article presents a mathematical overview of the methods that have shaped modern LLM post-training, with a particular emphasis on reinforcement learning approaches. We begin with policy gradient methods and derive the REINFORCE algorithm from first principles. Building upon this foundation, we introduce Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), the algorithm that established reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) as a practical paradigm for aligning language models. We then discuss Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), which eliminates the need for an explicit value function by leveraging relative comparisons among multiple generated responses. Next, we examine On-Policy Distillation (OPD) which aims to provide finer-grained, token-level learning signals while preserving the advantages of on-policy optimization. Finally, we consider Direct Preference Optimization (DPO). Although DPO is not a reinforcement learning algorithm in the traditional sense, it has emerged as an important alternative for post-training language models using preference data without explicit reward modeling or policy optimization. Together, these methods provide a broad view of the evolving landscape of LLM post-training and highlight the diverse strategies currently being explored to improve the reasoning capabilities of large language models.
